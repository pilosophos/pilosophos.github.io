I've been using the v1 MNT Pocket Reform for several months now, as a gift from ~vilmibm over a tilde.town, who had bought it when it was still being funded on Crowdsupply. The current version being sold has a better CPU and GPU than the one I have, so if you're thinking of buying new, your experience will be different, performance wise. 

I mainly use the Pocket Reform for simple things like browsing the web, SSHing into servers, and writing reviews about the MNT Pocket Reform while I'm out and about, and I don't need any serious computation. I also use it to drive my 3D printer with the Klipper firmware, which it handles perfectly. In my experience, the NXP i.MX 8M Plus compute module that includes the CPU and GPU is powerful enough to emulate my library of SNES games with SNES9x on Retroarch, but Firefox is noticeably sluggish when I've got a few Reddit tabs open.

The biggest draw of the Pocket Reform is the form factor: it's a whole Linux laptop fitted into a durable aluminum brick, with a mechanical keyboard and trackball mouse, that still manages to be light and small enough to take in a bag and use it on the train. When it's your stop, you can just stand up and keep using it, since the keyboard is operable with just your thumbs as you clutch the laptop in front of you with both hands. The Pocket Reform won't literally fit in your pocket, but it's still very convient.

To make the fully mechanical keyboard work in such a small size, it uses smaller than normal keycaps, an ortholinear layout, and a Hyper key used to access keys that didn't physically make it on the keyboard. Once I got used to the ortholinear layout, the typing feels wonderful, thanks to the Kailh Choc White switches, and the smaller keycap sizes aren't too noticeable.

My biggest gripe with the keyboard, however, is that typing some symbols can get really obtuse. There are no square bracket keys, so to type `[`, for instance, you have to press `Hyper+O`, and to type `~`, you have to press `Hyper+Shift+Esc` *twice*. The legends are pretty minimal, so I keep having to open the manual to remind me which Hyper combinations correspond to missing keys.

At the bottom of the keyboard is a trackball mouse with two mouse buttons on each side, not only for left and right click, but for middle click and scrolling with the ball. These buttons use the same keyswitches and caps as the keyboard, so they feel very good. The trackball is very smooth and tracks nicely, although mouse-heavy applications like creating PowerPoints would probably be a huge pain.

Speaking of the trackball, the trackball sensor was one of the parts I had to get replaced under warranty when I initially received it, along with a charge board that released its magic smoke when I charged the Pocket Reform overnight. The charge board breakage was a known issue in earlier production runs and MNT were sending out replacements at no charge. I asked if they could throw in a new trackball sensor as well since mine was unusably bad, and they obliged! It took several weeks to actually ship, but the support process was quite painless, and the replacements have been working perfectly for months.

Installing the replacements is also quite painless, as one of the selling points of the Pocket Reform is its repairability. You can take this thing apart with just a small Phillips-head screwdriver, and the manual tells you what all the parts are and how they fit together. You can buy install upgraded compute modules, an NVMe SSD, a 4G modem, and even Qwiic I2C sensors yourself if you're so inclined.

I've seen someone upgrade to much larger batteries that can last 10 hours on a single charge, and I'm inclined to try this myself, since the stock batteries last only 3 hours or so under moderate load. Even when it's off, the batteries will discharge in a day or so, making it the fastest draining battery I've ever owned.

There's actually a switch on the side of the machine, toggleable with a SIM card ejector, that disconnects the charge board from the motherboard. Despite that, the batteries will still discharge within several days, which I find rather bizarre.

Putting aside the poor battery life, the Pocket Reform has been pretty good for my use case of light computation when I'm out running errands or at a convention. While the small screen can seem cramped at times, especially when visiting sites that insist you join their mailing list or chat with their soulless support bots, I'd rather be using this than my smartphone for just about everything.

Outside of pure usability, I also just think this device is just kind of fascinating. Other people seem to think so as well, because every one of my friends (and occasionally some strangers) point out the funny little device when I see it for the first time and ask what it is.
